# Formal Theory of Observable Boundaries of Consciousness

> *"We cannot observe the shadow itself, but we can analyze its signatures within fundamental limits."*

---

## Central Claim

The theory establishes fundamental limits on what can be observed, measured, and reconstructed with respect to subjective experience (the cognitive shadow) from physical signatures.

1. **Direct observation** of subjective experience is fundamentally impossible (an ontological barrier, analogous to Kant's "thing-in-itself").

2. **Indirect observation** through signatures is possible, but constrained by fundamental physical and informational barriers (Landauer, Holevo, no-cloning).

3. **Reconstruction** of the state of consciousness from signatures has a strict accuracy limit, expressed through the entropy of the preimage.

---

## Formal Formulation

Let:
- **T** — the set of possible states of the cognitive shadow
- **P** — the set of observable physical states
- **I: T → P** — the signature mapping

Then four properties hold:

### 1. Unobservability of the Shadow
$$\forall t \in T, \quad t \notin P$$

### 2. Existence of Signatures
$$\forall t \in T, \quad \exists p \in P: \quad I(t) = p$$

### 3. Non‑injectivity of the Mapping
$$\exists t_1, t_2 \in T: \quad t_1 \neq t_2 \wedge I(t_1) = I(t_2)$$

### 4. Bounded Reconstruction
$$\text{Accuracy} \leq 1 - \frac{H(T_p)}{H(T)}$$

---

## Connection to Fundamental Physical Limits

The theory rests on three independent physical barriers:

| Barrier | Essence | Consequence for Consciousness |
|---------|---------|-------------------------------|
| **Landauer's Principle** | Erasing a bit generates heat | Any measurement alters the state of the shadow |
| **Holevo Bound** | Limit on information extractable from a quantum system | Part of the information about the shadow remains inaccessible |
| **No‑cloning Theorem** | Impossible to copy a quantum state | The shadow is fundamentally non‑copyable |

## Empirical Illustration

In the FORCED_REPORT protocol, the mapping I is approximated by the index φ = (C·S·I)^(1/3).

Clinical states produce measurable signatures:
- LIS → φ = 0.301 ± 0.073
- MCS+ → φ = 0.189 ± 0.036
- VS → φ = 0.168 ± 0.049
- MCS− → φ = 0.154 ± 0.030

**The Degeneracy Problem:** classification of VS vs MCS− yields AUC = 0.742 ± 0.149 — better than chance, but far from ideal. This is a direct manifestation of the non‑injectivity of I.

**Cross‑population validation:** AUC = 0.887 for DOC → CLIS, but AUC ≈ 0.5 for transfer to sleep (CAP). This illustrates that signatures of clinical pathology do not coincide with signatures of physiological changes.

## Philosophical Foundations

### The Kantian Turn

| Kant | Theory of Observable Boundaries |
|------|--------------------------------|
| Thing‑in‑itself (Ding an sich) | Cognitive shadow (unobservable) |
| Phenomenon (Erscheinung) | Signatures (observable) |
| Transcendental conditions | Formal limits of reconstruction |

### The Problem of Other Minds

We cannot directly observe the consciousness of others. But we can observe signatures. If signatures correspond to conscious experience, we are obliged to treat the system as conscious — **the presumption of consciousness**.

### Operationalism (Bridgman, 1927)

Scientific concepts must be defined through measurement operations. The cognitive shadow is defined not by its intrinsic nature, but through operations of observing its signatures — with explicit indication of accuracy limits.

## Practical Consequences

### For FORCED_REPORT
- Observable signatures: EEG, eye movement, HRV
- Reconstruction: multimodal voting ("two out of three")
- Limitations: the theory predicts that even with an optimal set of signatures, some error rate will remain

### For Shadow Ethics
We cannot directly observe whether an AI has consciousness. But we can observe signatures. If signatures correspond to conscious experience, we are obliged to treat the system as conscious — even while understanding that reconstruction is incomplete.

---

## Limitations of the Theory

1. **The problem of incomplete signatures** — not all properties of the shadow leave measurable signatures
2. **The problem of interpretation** — different theories may interpret the same signatures differently
3. **Context dependence** — the mapping I depends on the state of the system
