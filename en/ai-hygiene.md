# Guide to Conscious AI Use

> *“Where your effort ends, your intellect ends.”*

---

## What this guide is about

A practical guide to developing cognitive abilities and avoiding their degradation when using generative AI.

Based on 2024–2026 research (MIT Media Lab, Wharton School, BCG, Nature) and connected to cognitive shadow theory through the principle: **consciousness is not just a “level of activity” — it is a complex integrative process that can be disrupted in different ways.**

---

## The AI paradox

Digital tools optimize short‑term task performance at the cost of potential long‑term cognitive effort needed to sustain independent thinking.

**Studies show:**
- ChatGPT users show reduced activity in brain areas associated with memory (MIT Media Lab, 2025)
- Higher confidence in the tool’s ability correlates with “less critical thinking effort” (Microsoft, 2025)
- On‑demand AI assistance undermines “productive struggle” — cognitively demanding work essential for long‑term skill development (Wharton School, 2026)
- 14% of users report symptoms of “AI brain fry” — cognitive burnout from excessive AI use (BCG, 2026)

## 5 golden rules

### Rule 1: AI is a tool, not an author

❌ **Don’t:** “Write an essay on… 500 words, conclusion.”

✅ **Do:** “Here are my theses. Find logical gaps, ask 3 counter‑questions, propose an alternative viewpoint.”

### Rule 2: Cognitive friction is necessary

❌ **Don’t:** “Give a short summary without examples or context.”

✅ **Do:** “Break down the concept layer by layer. Leave key formulations for me to rephrase myself. Point out where beginners most often make mistakes.”

### Rule 3: Speed ≠ understanding

❌ **Don’t:** “Fix all errors in the text.”

✅ **Do:** “Point out lines with errors or stylistic issues. Explain the rule. I will fix it myself.”

### Rule 4: You first — then the machine

❌ **Don’t:** “Check if my hypothesis is correct.”

✅ **Do:** “Play the skeptic. Find 3 weak points in my argument and propose an experiment to test it.”

### Rule 5: Sovereignty of attention

❌ **Don’t:** Any query that hands over control to the algorithm

✅ **Do:** Always retain final decision‑making, pace, and depth of processing. Start with your own draft.

## Checklist: 7 signs of cognitive degradation

Mark each item that occurs regularly (over the last 2–3 weeks):

1. ☐ I accept the first AI answer as truth without clarification
2. ☐ I avoid texts longer than 3–5 pages
3. ☐ I can’t retell material in my own words
4. ☐ Basic spelling errors become habitual
5. ☐ I feel anxious when AI is unavailable
6. ☐ I have stopped writing drafts without autocomplete
7. ☐ I use AI to confirm my own views

**Scoring:**
- 7–14 points: 🟢 Low level — excellent condition
- 15–21 points: 🟡 Medium — do a light detox protocol
- 22–28 points: 🔴 High — do a standard protocol
- 29–35 points: ⚠️ Critical — do an intensive protocol

## Cognitive detox protocol

### 🟢 Light (2 days per week)

**Prohibited:**
- Generation of texts, code, plans by AI
- Using AI for decision‑making
- Autocomplete and autofill

**Allowed:**
- Fact‑checking (with verification via 1–2 sources)
- Spelling checks (with rule explanations)

**Daily practice:**
- 30 minutes of deep reading without digital devices
- Written reflection at the end of the day
- Metacognitive pause before each AI use

### 🟡 Standard (7–14 days)

**Prohibited:**
- ALL forms of generative AI
- Search engines with AI summaries
- Auto‑translation and auto‑correction

**Allowed:**
- Classic search engines for accessing primary sources
- Dictionaries and reference works (without AI summaries)

**Daily practice:**
- 45–60 minutes of deep reading
- Daily writing without technology (at least 300 words)
- Memory training: retelling after reading

### 🔴 Intensive (14–21 days)

**Prohibited (absolutely everything):**
- Complete abstinence from generative AI in all forms
- Complete abstinence from search engines with AI components
- Complete abstinence from autocomplete and auto‑correction

**Allowed:**
- Paper books, magazines, newspapers
- Handwriting
- Offline dictionaries, encyclopedias
- Live communication with people
- Nature, physical activity

**Daily practice (minimum):**
- 2 hours of deep reading without digital devices
- 1 hour of handwriting
- 30 minutes of cognitive exercises

## Exercises for maintaining neuroplasticity

| Exercise | How to do it | Neurobiological goal |
|----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Blind retelling** | Study text → close AI → retell from memory → compare with original | Activate retrieval practice |
| **Devil’s advocate** | Formulate a thesis. AI provides 3 counter‑arguments. You respond to each | Loosen confirmation bias |
| **Manual verification** | After receiving an AI answer, find 3 primary sources. Check each fact | Directly combat AI hallucinations |
| **Long reading** | 20 min/day: complex text from a different field. Take notes. Retell | Train “cognitive patience” |
| **Prompt reflection** | Keep a journal for each AI session | Metacognitive control |
| **De‑automate the highway** | Ban professional jargon. Describe the task as if to a 12‑year‑old | Break dominant neural pathways |
| **Metacognitive pause** | Before turning to AI, stop for 60 seconds. Answer 3 questions: “What do I want to know?”, “What do I already know?”, “What is the main question?” | Activate your own neural bridges before consulting AI |

## Connection to cognitive shadow theory

Our empirical research shows that the index φ, reflecting the formalizability of consciousness, is specific to clinical disorders and does not generalize to sleep. This underscores that consciousness is not just a “level of activity” but a complex integrative process that can be disrupted in different ways.

Similarly, in everyday AI use we must distinguish:
- **Temporary reduction in cognitive load** (sleep, rest) — normal and necessary
- **Systemic degradation of thinking skills** (AI dependence, metacognitive laziness) — a pathology requiring intervention

Rules 2 and 4 are aimed precisely at preventing systemic degradation, not at rejecting rest.

## Conclusion

AI can be your co‑pilot, your trainer, your opponent — anything you want it to be. But it should never be your thinking “self.”

Because where autopilot begins, the pilot ends.
