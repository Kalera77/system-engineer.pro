# AI Hygiene Guide

> "Where your effort ends, your intellect ends."

## What this guide is about

This is a practical guide to developing cognitive abilities and avoiding their degradation when using generative AI.

It is based on 2024–2026 research (MIT Media Lab, Wharton School, BCG, Nature) and is linked to the cognitive shadow theory through the principle: **consciousness is not just a "level of activity" but a complex integration process that can be disrupted in different ways**.

## The AI paradox

Digital tools optimise short-term task performance at the expense of long-term cognitive effort necessary for maintaining independent thinking.

**Studies show:**
- ChatGPT users show reduced activity in brain regions associated with memory (MIT Media Lab, 2025)
- Higher confidence in the tool's ability to perform a task correlates with "less critical thinking effort" (Microsoft, 2025)
- On‑demand AI help undermines "productive struggle" — cognitively demanding work of overcoming challenges (Wharton School, 2026)
- 14% of users report symptoms of "AI brain fry" — cognitive burnout from excessive AI use (BCG, 2026)

## 5 Golden Rules

### Rule 1: AI is a tool, not an author

❌ **Don't:** "Write an essay on… 500 words, conclusion."

✅ **Do:** "Here are my theses. Find logical gaps, ask 3 counter‑questions, suggest an alternative point of view."

### Rule 2: Cognitive friction is necessary

❌ **Don't:** "Give a short summary without examples and context."

✅ **Do:** "Break down the concept layer by layer. Leave key formulations for me to rephrase independently. Indicate where beginners most often make mistakes."

### Rule 3: Speed ≠ understanding

❌ **Don't:** "Fix all errors in the text."

✅ **Do:** "Point out lines with errors or stylistic issues. Explain the rule. I will fix it myself."

### Rule 4: You first, then the machine

❌ **Don't:** "Check if my hypothesis is correct."

✅ **Do:** "Play the sceptic. Find 3 weak points in my argument and propose an experiment to test it."

### Rule 5: Sovereignty of attention

❌ **Don't:** Any query that hands control to the algorithm.

✅ **Do:** Always retain final decision, pace, and depth of processing. Start with your own draft.

## Checklist: 7 signs of cognitive degradation

Mark each item that appears regularly (over the last 2–3 weeks):

1. ☐ I accept the first AI answer as truth without clarification
2. ☐ I avoid texts longer than 3–5 pages
3. ☐ I cannot paraphrase material in my own words
4. ☐ Basic grammatical errors become habitual
5. ☐ I feel anxious when AI is unavailable
6. ☐ I stopped writing drafts without autocomplete
7. ☐ I use AI to confirm my own views

**Scoring:**
- 7–14 points: 🟢 Low level — excellent condition
- 15–21 points: 🟡 Medium — do the light detox protocol
- 22–28 points: 🔴 High — do the standard protocol
- 29–35 points: ⚠️ Critical — do the intensive protocol

## Cognitive detox protocol

### 🟢 Light (2 days per week)

**Prohibited:**
- Generating texts, code, plans with AI
- Using AI for decision making
- Autocomplete and autofill

**Allowed:**
- Fact‑checking (verifying through 1–2 sources)
- Spelling checks (with explanations of rules)

**Daily practice:**
- 30 minutes of deep reading without digital devices
- Written reflection at the end of the day
- Metacognitive pause before each AI use

### 🟡 Standard (7–14 days)

**Prohibited:**
- ALL forms of generative AI
- Search engines with AI‑generated summaries
- Auto‑translation and autocorrect

**Allowed:**
- Classic search engines to access primary sources
- Dictionaries and reference books (without AI summaries)

**Daily practice:**
- 45–60 minutes of deep reading
- Daily writing without technology (at least 300 words)
- Memory training: retell after reading

### 🔴 Intensive (14–21 days)

**Prohibited (absolutely everything):**
- Complete abstention from generative AI in all forms
- Complete abstention from search engines with AI components
- Complete abstention from autocomplete, autocorrect

**Allowed:**
- Paper books, journals, newspapers
- Handwriting
- Offline dictionaries, encyclopedias
- In‑person communication with people
- Nature, physical activity

**Daily practice (minimum):**
- 2 hours of deep reading without digital devices
- 1 hour of handwriting
- 30 minutes of cognitive exercises

## Brain trainers for maintaining neuroplasticity

| Exercise | How to do it | Neuroscientific goal |
|----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Blind retelling** | Study text → close AI → retell from memory → compare with original | Activate retrieval practice |
| **Devil's advocate** | State a thesis. AI gives 3 counter‑arguments. You respond to each | Disrupt confirmation bias |
| **Manual verification** | After getting an AI answer, find 3 primary sources. Check every fact | Direct counter to AI hallucinations |
| **Long reading** | 20 min/day: challenging text from another field. Take notes. Retell | Train "cognitive patience" |
| **Prompt reflection** | Keep a journal for each AI session | Metacognitive control |
| **De‑automating the default pathway** | Ban professional jargon. Describe the task as for a 12‑year‑old | Disrupt dominant cognitive pathways |
| **Metacognitive pause** | Before turning to AI, stop for 60 seconds. Answer 3 questions: "What do I want to know?", "What do I already know?", "What is the main question?" | Activate your own neural bridges |

## Connection to the cognitive shadow theory

Our empirical studies show that index φ, reflecting consciousness formalizability, is specific to clinical disorders and does not generalise to sleep. This underlines that consciousness is not just a "level of activity" but a complex integration process that can be disrupted in different ways.

Analogously, in everyday AI use we must distinguish:
- **Temporary reduction of cognitive load** (sleep, rest) — normal and necessary
- **Systemic degradation of thinking skills** (AI addiction, metacognitive laziness) — pathology requiring intervention

Rule 2 (Cognitive friction is necessary) and Rule 4 (You first, then the machine) are aimed precisely at preventing systemic degradation, not at denying rest.

## Conclusion

AI can be your co‑pilot, trainer, opponent — whatever you want it to be. But it should never be your thinking self.

Because where autopilot begins, the pilot ends.
